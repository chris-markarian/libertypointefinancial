5 Estate Planning Mistakes That Could Cost Your Family Everything

A Free Guide from Liberty Pointe Financial
Complimentary — No Purchase Required
Introduction

Most people put off estate planning because it feels complicated, expensive, or like something only wealthy people need. The truth is, if you own a home, have savings, or have people who depend on you — you need an estate plan. And the mistakes families make when they don't have one (or have the wrong one) can cost their loved ones tens of thousands of dollars, years of legal headaches, and enormous emotional pain.

This guide covers the five most common and costly mistakes we see — and what you can do about each one.

01
Mistake #1

Relying on a Will Alone

A will is better than nothing — but far too many families believe that having a will means their estate is "handled." It isn't. Here's the painful truth most people don't know:

  • Wills go through probate — a court-supervised legal process that is public, slow, and expensive.
  • A will does not avoid probate. It simply tells the probate court what you wanted — and then the court decides how and when to honor it.
  • Probate proceedings can take 9 months to 2+ years and consume 3–7% of your estate's value in legal and court fees.
  • Everything filed in probate becomes a matter of public record — including your assets, debts, and beneficiaries.
⚠ The Hidden Cost

A $400,000 estate passing through probate can easily lose $20,000–$28,000 in court and attorney fees — money that was meant for your family.

✅
The Solution

A revocable living trust keeps your assets private and transfers them directly and immediately to your heirs — no court, no waiting, no public record, no unnecessary cost.

02
Mistake #2

Ignoring Beneficiary Designations

Your IRA, 401(k), and life insurance policies do not follow your will. They go to whoever is named as beneficiary on the account itself — period. No exceptions. A will cannot override a beneficiary designation.

This means if you set up a life insurance policy when you were 28 and never updated it, the named beneficiary gets the money. Full stop. Even if your will says something completely different.

⚠ Common Disasters We See

Ex-spouse still named. Your divorce didn't remove them from your 401(k).

Deceased parent named. The money goes into a court process to sort out the estate.

Minor child named. Courts appoint a guardian to manage the funds until age 18 — exactly what most parents wanted to avoid.

✅
The Solution

Review all beneficiary designations every 2–3 years and immediately after every major life event — marriage, divorce, birth, or death. This one step alone can prevent enormous heartache.

03
Mistake #3

Not Planning for Incapacity

Estate planning isn't only about what happens when you die. What happens if you're alive but unable to make decisions — due to an accident, stroke, or serious illness?

Without the right legal documents in place, a court will decide who manages your medical care and finances. That process is called guardianship or conservatorship — and it's one of the most costly, invasive, and emotionally draining experiences a family can go through.

$5,000–$10,000+
Typical cost of court-supervised guardianship proceedings — plus months of delays
  • Courts don't automatically appoint your spouse or child. It must be petitioned and approved.
  • During the process, financial accounts can be frozen and medical decisions delayed.
  • Ongoing court oversight may be required for years — at ongoing legal expense.
✅
The Solution

Two documents — a healthcare directive (living will) and a durable financial power of attorney — eliminate this entire problem. They take about an hour to set up and ensure the people you trust are empowered to act on your behalf, immediately, without court involvement.

04
Mistake #4

Owning Property Without a Trust

You might have a trust. But if your real estate isn't titled in the name of the trust, it doesn't matter — that property will still go through probate when you pass away.

And here's where it gets worse: real estate goes through probate in the state where it's located. That means if you own property in multiple states, your family faces multiple probate proceedings — one in each state.

  • A vacation cabin in another state? That's a second probate. A second court. A second set of legal fees.
  • Investment property? Same story.
  • Even a small piece of land requires full probate if it's not in your trust.
🏠 Real Situation

"They had a trust — but their Utah home and Idaho cabin were never transferred into it. When they passed, the family had to open probate in both states. It took 14 months and cost over $18,000 in fees that could have been completely avoided."

✅
The Solution

Transfer all titled assets — real estate, vehicles, bank accounts — into your revocable living trust. This is called "funding" the trust, and it's the step most families miss even when they've done everything else right.

05
Mistake #5

Waiting Until It's Too Late

This is the most heartbreaking mistake of all — because it's entirely avoidable.

Estate planning requires legal mental capacity. If you wait until a health crisis has already impaired your cognition, it may be too late to legally create a valid estate plan. Courts can and do reject documents signed during periods of diminished capacity. And there's no "emergency override" — once that window closes, it's closed.

📖 Real Story

"Dad kept saying he was going to get to it. He had a stroke at 71 — and while he survived, his capacity was compromised. By the time the family realized there was no estate plan in place, it was legally too late to create one. The result: 18 months in probate court and over $40,000 in legal fees — for an estate that could have been structured in an afternoon."

  • A diagnosis doesn't have to be terminal to disqualify you from estate planning.
  • Dementia, stroke, and even certain medications can legally affect capacity.
  • Challenges to "deathbed" documents are common — and often successful.
✅
The Solution

The best time to do an estate plan is when you don't need it yet. Not after a diagnosis. Not after a birthday that "feels like a milestone." Now — while you have clarity, capacity, and time to do it right.

You Don't Need to Be Wealthy to Need a Plan

You just need people — or things — worth protecting.

Estate planning isn't about being rich. It's about being responsible. It's about making sure that when something happens to you — and something will, eventually — the people you love aren't left cleaning up a legal and financial mess during the worst time of their lives.

The five mistakes in this guide are common. They're costly. And every single one of them is preventable with a little planning and the right guidance.
